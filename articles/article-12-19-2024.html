Dont Learn Prompt Engineering !

AI is great. We all love it. But not many are using it.

This is because, for newcomers, AI feels odd and slippery. People often give it one chance, and if it disappoints, they never return.

This reaction is common because LLMs are surprisingly hard to get a handle on.

Most try to use it like a simple search box, asking direct questions such as, Could switching to hybrid work models reduce staff burnout? or What methods could help a regional retailer increase foot traffic? And theres nothing wrong with that.

When an LLM can connect to the internet, it might do an acceptable job answering these. However, only a small portion of medical professionals, for example, realize that they can paste an entire patient case history into the chatbot and ask for a full analysis. Most users do not even try this approach.

Many people who test AI once have trouble when they come back, mainly struggling to figure out what to do next. Some say that it writes generic text, and others sit frozen, not knowing what to request. In short, they cant prompt the AI.

One of the most promoted solutions to these problems is to learn Prompt Engineering, - aa skill set that enables you to write prompts in a way that AI can easily understand, ensuring that you receive the desired output. Yet this idea can push people away. It sends the message that you need specialized knowledge to unlock the benefits of AI. Telling someone they must learn complex instructions before they begin can make AI feel off-limits to ordinary users.

Some like to frame prompt engineering as a real science with clear rules, but that is not accurate. There is no rigid set of rules for prompting AI. It does not follow a neat script. Small changes in spacing or formatting might alter results. Sometimes asking it to read the question again helps, and polite wording can improve its performance. Meanwhile, larger and newer models react less to tiny details, making it easier to get good results without expert-level guidance. Some tools even help rewrite your prompt, which reduces pressure on you to find the perfect wording.

Of course, anyone can learn about prompts and study how these large language models work. We even created an article about some of the mental models that you can use for prompt engineering. However, that is not mandatory for everyone.

Most people simply need enough hands-on time to get comfortable. By experimenting with the AI in tasks you care about, you will quickly gain a sense of what it can do and where it fails. Real experience counts more than memorizing prompt tricks.

Test on what you already know

Treating AI like an intern often leads to narrow usage. In truth, an AI models can be more capable than any beginner you would hire. Better to think bigger.

Instead of forcing it into a small box, try using it widely in your daily work. Test it on tasks you know well so you can easily spot when it is mistaken. Expect it to occasionally talk nonsense. This is normal. Over time, you learn which tasks cause it to slip up and which ones let it shine. You can lower the chances of hallucinations by telling it to admit ignorance when uncertain. Simple instructions like, If you do not have enough information, say so, can greatly improve its honesty.

At the same time, you must communicate clearly. Instead of asking vaguely for Give me a unique marketing plan specify exactly what you need: Create a marketing plan for a small coffee shop in a busy downtown area. Focus on attracting morning commuters with fresh pastry options and promotional discounts during peak office hours.

You can provide examples of what good answers look like, outline steps you want it to follow, and correct it when it goes off track. If it seems confused, tell it to ask questions for clarification. This mutual exchange helps shape better outcomes.

Another key point is that AI never gets irritated. You can ask it to try again and again without wearing it down. Instead of accepting the first result that IA gives you, ask Give me three versions with different tones, or List thirty ideas, not just five. You become a kind of curator, nudging it to try different angles, mix concepts from previous attempts, or push further into new territory.

For instance, if you see a decent idea at item 8 on a list of possibilities, you can ask for more ideas like that one or mix it with another concept you liked. This back-and-forth process can create results you never thought possible.

Helps in your thinking

Sometimes, just talking through your thoughts can clarify your mind, similar to how programmers use a rubber duck to explain tricky problems.

You might not gain brilliant insights from the AIs responses, but the act of explaining your concerns and bouncing them off a neutral system can help you find your own answers.

The less you treat it like a stiff machine, the more natural it feels.

Dont make this hard

None of this must be painful. The most important step is simply to use AI. Set a small goal: spend about ten hours using an AI chatbot on tasks that matter to you. By the time you have that experience, you will understand how AI fits into your work and life. You will have a sense of what prompts get good results and which ones do not.

You will get a sense of what prompts lead to results you value. You will see its limits and its strengths. You do not need to aim for some perfect understanding. Just start. Over time, your skill will grow naturally, and AI will shift from a strange gadget into a familiar tool you can use with confidence.
